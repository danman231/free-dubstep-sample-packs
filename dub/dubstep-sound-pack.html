Frequently Asked Questions

Who gets paid for a sample?

When a sample of music, sound, or any other form of media is used in a new work, the original creator or copyright holder of the sampled material typically gets paid. This payment comes in the form of licensing fees or royalties, which are negotiated before the sample is used. The specifics of the payment can vary greatly depending on the popularity of the sample, the way it's being used, and the agreement between the parties involved. In some cases, the artist using the sample might also agree to give a percentage of their song's earnings to the original creator.

Can you sample dead artists?

Yes, you can sample music from dead artists, but it's important to understand that the legality of doing so depends on copyright laws, which do not expire upon the artist's death. Copyrights in music are typically held for 70 years after the death of the last surviving author (in many jurisdictions). Therefore, to legally sample music from a deceased artist, you generally need to obtain permission from the copyright holder, which could be the artist's estate, a record label, or another entity that has acquired the rights. Failure to obtain the necessary permissions can result in legal action, including lawsuits for copyright infringement.

Do rappers get permission to sample?

Rappers, like other musicians, typically need to obtain permission to sample someone else's music in their tracks. This process involves clearing the sample, which means getting approval from the copyright holders of the original song, which could include the songwriter, performer, and record label. Failure to get proper clearance can lead to legal issues, including copyright infringement lawsuits. However, some artists choose to use samples without permission, either hoping not to be noticed or considering the potential legal battle worth the risk.
