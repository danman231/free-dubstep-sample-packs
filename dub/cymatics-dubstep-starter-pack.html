Frequently Asked Questions

Is sampling music a crime?

Sampling music without permission or proper licensing can be considered copyright infringement, which is a form of intellectual property theft and thus can be treated as a crime or a civil offense depending on the jurisdiction. Artists and producers often need to obtain clearance for using samples from the original copyright holders, which usually involves agreeing on terms and possibly paying for the rights to use the music. However, some uses of samples may fall under fair use provisions, depending on factors like the amount of the original music used and the purpose of the sampling. Therefore, whether sampling music is a crime depends on the legal context and whether the sampler has obtained the necessary permissions.

Why is it called Brostep?

Brostep is a subgenre of dubstep that emerged in the United States around the late 2000s and early 2010s. It is called "Brostep" because it was popularized and primarily consumed by the "bro" culture, which refers to a subculture of young men who are often associated with partying, fraternity life, and a particular macho attitude. The term "Brostep" is somewhat pejorative and was used to differentiate this style from the original UK Dubstep sound, which is deeper and more minimalistic. Brostep is characterized by its aggressive basslines, high energy levels, and complex, syncopated rhythms, appealing to a broader and more mainstream audience.

Why are sample packs so expensive?

Sample packs can be expensive due to several factors including the cost of production, licensing fees, and the value of the content they provide. Creating high-quality samples involves professional recording equipment, skilled musicians, and producers, all of which contribute to the production costs. Additionally, if the samples include copyrighted material, licensing fees may be required to legally distribute them. Lastly, sample packs offer a convenient and valuable resource for music producers, allowing them to save time and enhance their productions, which justifies a higher price point.

Is EDM the same as dubstep?

No, EDM (Electronic Dance Music) is not the same as dubstep. EDM is a broad category that encompasses a wide range of electronic music genres, including house, techno, trance, and indeed, dubstep among others. Dubstep, on the other hand, is a specific genre within the EDM umbrella, characterized by its heavy bass and intricate rhythms. While dubstep is a part of EDM, not all EDM can be classified as dubstep.

What makes dubstep dubstep?

Dubstep is characterized by its heavy use of bass and sub-bass frequencies, typically ranging from 140 beats per minute (BPM). It often features a half-time rhythm, where the percussion will give the effect of being slower than it actually is, creating a distinctive syncopated feel. The genre is also known for its emphasis on the "drop," a moment in the track where the tension built up by the intro and build-up is released with a heavy and often complex bassline. These elements combined create the unique sound that defines dubstep.
