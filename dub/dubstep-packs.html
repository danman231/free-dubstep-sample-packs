Can you get sued for chord progressions?

Generally, you cannot get sued solely for using a chord progression in your music. Chord progressions are considered a fundamental element of music theory and are not protected by copyright law, as they are too basic and widely used to be owned by anyone. However, if your use of a chord progression closely mimics a specific, recognizable aspect of another song, including melody, lyrics, or unique rhythm, in a way that could be considered copying or derivative, then you might face legal challenges. It's the unique combination of elements in a song that can be protected, not the chord progression by itself.

Deadmau5, whose real name is Joel Zimmerman, is primarily known for his work in the progressive house and electro house genres. While he has experimented with a variety of electronic music styles, dubstep is not a genre he is widely recognized for producing. Deadmau5 has been vocal about his opinions on various music genres, including dubstep, but his discography mainly showcases his talents in house music and other electronic subgenres rather than focusing on dubstep.

Can you use 30 seconds of a copyrighted song?

Yes, you can use 30 seconds of a copyrighted song, but it's not automatically free from copyright infringement just because it's a short duration. The myth of a specific "safe" duration, such as 30 seconds, is widespread but incorrect. Whether the use of a copyrighted song's segment is permissible depends on factors like the purpose of use, the amount used in relation to the whole, and its effect on the market value of the original work, often considered under the principles of fair use in jurisdictions like the United States. Always seek permission or consult a legal expert to avoid infringement issues.
