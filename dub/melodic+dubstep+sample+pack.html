What are you legally allowed to sample?

Legally, you are allowed to sample copyrighted material under certain conditions that fall under the doctrine of fair use. This includes using small portions of a work for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research. However, the legality of sampling also depends on factors like the amount of the work used, the nature of the copyrighted work, the effect on the market value of the original work, and the purpose of the use. It's important to note that these rules can vary by country, and in some cases, explicit permission from the copyright holder may be required to avoid legal issues.

What is the most sampled song in history?

The most sampled song in history is "Amen, Brother" by The Winstons, a B-side of the 1969 single "Color Him Father." The six-second drum break in the middle of the song, known as the "Amen Break," has been extensively sampled and reused in various genres, especially in hip hop, drum and bass, and jungle music. Its widespread use has made it a foundational element in the development of these genres, although for many years, the original artists did not receive royalties or recognition for its ubiquitous use. The "Amen Break" is not just a piece of music history but a cornerstone in the evolution of electronic and sampled music.

Do sampled artists get royalties?

The specifics of the royalty payments, such as the amount and how they are calculated, can vary depending on the agreement between the parties involved.
